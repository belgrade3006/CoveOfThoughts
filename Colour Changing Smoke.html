Colour Changing Smoke

Most of us are aware of Rayleigh’s Law of Scattering since maybe the tenth standard or before. It’s stated as light of shorter wavelength is scattered the most, by the atmospheric particles. Now the word ‘atmospheric particles’ is extremely vague to scientific minds. What are those particles? What special quality do they possess for which they scatter light in such a way? These are the questions that should have thronged our minds. Rather, that’s what our teachers expected. But they never did. At least not to me.

Coming back, ‘atmospheric particles’ refers only to those particles whose dimensions were much smaller than the wavelength of the scattered ray. So according to Rayleigh’s Law of Scattering, the violet end of the visible spectrum should be scattered the most. In the presence of the sun, the sky appears blue since the wavelength corresponding to blue light, which is much shorter compared to red, is scattered the most. Again, there was a loophole which my unintellectual mind had missed when we were taught. Going by the colours of the rainbow, violet light is what should’ve been scattered most. Why in the world, then, does the sky appear blue and not violet? This itself answers why my statement regarding the scattering of blue light was scientifically incorrect. Of course, a law should be universal. It is. Violet indeed does get scattered the most. But in reality, all colours in the visible spectrum — in fact, the entire electromagnetic spectrum whose wavelengths are larger than the particle’s dimensions — are scattered. Since our eyes can detect only the visible range of the spectrum, we discard those which don’t stimulate our eyes. The inverse proportionality doesn’t imply it’s only the violet light that gets scattered and not the red or yellow light. Light of every colour gets scattered. Just that the rate of scattering is maximum for violet, a little lesser for blue and so on.

So, the light that we perceive as the colour of the sky is the mean of all the colours in the visible range, with more weight in the lower wavelength region, which brings the mean to the blue of the sky that we observe.

Think of light as photons. There is a light beam, with equal intensities of different colours aimed at a scattering particle. Now you enclose the setup in a hollow shell and collect photons at the periphery, at different positions. You’ll get the maximum number of photons corresponding to violet. But you’ll also receive handfuls of red and orange too. If you take the average of all wavelengths, the wavelength of the blue of the sky is what you’ll perceive.

At dawn, we view the sky as reddish orange for the same reason. We perceive only those light rays which travel the longest distance in the atmosphere without being scattered. According to the law we’re obeying, red light should travel the longest distance unscattered. But the mean of all the light we perceive shows us the vermilion of sunset.

Can we extend this law to clouds too? For the application, the size condition must be satisfied. And in this case, it is not, unfortunately. Clouds are made up of moisture particles much larger than the ‘atmospheric particles’ which scatter lights of all frequencies equally. Hence the light we receive from clouds is actually a mixture of all colours, which indeed is white.

But are all clouds white? What about nimbus clouds? Of course they’re grey. When the cloud is leaden with moisture, as nimbus clouds are, they absorb some part of the light and scatter the rest. The mean therefore shifts from white to a shade of grey.

All my enthusiasm about scattering of light was ignited when I got hold of the book For the Love of Physics by Professor Walter Lewin. There he narrates an experiment to demonstrate the scattering of light. By turning off all the spotlights and aiming a strong spotlight at the ceiling, he lights a few cigarettes and holds them in the light beam. The smoke particles are small enough to cause Rayleigh scattering and hence the students observe blue smoke. Then, as an extrapolation to his demonstration, he inhales the smoke, holds his breath for about a minute and exhales. The students now see white smoke. The tiny smoke particles grow in his lungs where there’s an abundance of moisture and thus, when exhaled, the smoke appears white. Colour changing smoke. Ingenious, isn’t it?
